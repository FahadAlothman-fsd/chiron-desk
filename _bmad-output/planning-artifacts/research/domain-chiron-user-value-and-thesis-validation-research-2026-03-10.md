## Regulatory Requirements

### Applicable Regulations

For Chiron's domain (AI-enabled developer workflows with telemetry and surveys), the primary legal regimes are privacy/data protection law and emerging AI governance law. In the EU, GDPR is the baseline framework for any personal data processing, including product analytics and in-app questionnaires. The EU AI Act introduces risk-based obligations for AI systems and creates a forward-looking compliance requirement for providers/deployers operating in EU contexts. In the UK, organizations should follow UK GDPR and ICO AI guidance, including explainability and risk-assessment expectations.
_Source: https://data.europa.eu/eli/reg/2016/679/oj_
_Source: https://data.europa.eu/eli/reg/2024/1689/oj_
_Source: https://ico.org.uk/for-organisations/uk-gdpr-guidance-and-resources/artificial-intelligence/_

### Industry Standards and Best Practices

NIST AI RMF 1.0 is a high-value governance baseline for trustworthy AI lifecycle management, and NIST's GenAI profile (AI 600-1) helps operationalize generative-AI-specific risks. For information security governance expected by enterprise customers, ISO/IEC 27001:2022 is a common best-practice anchor. These are generally voluntary standards, but they strongly influence procurement and trust outcomes.
_Source: https://www.nist.gov/itl/ai-risk-management-framework_
_Source: https://www.nist.gov/news-events/news/2024/07/nist-releases-first-iteration-its-genai-profile_
_Source: https://www.iso.org/isoiec-27001-information-security.html_

### Compliance Frameworks

A practical framework for Chiron should combine: (1) legal compliance controls (GDPR/CCPA rights and lawful processing), (2) AI-risk governance controls (NIST AI RMF style), and (3) security governance controls (ISO 27001-style ISMS). For California users, CCPA/CPRA rights handling should be built into data operations, with active oversight by California enforcement bodies.

### Data Protection and Privacy

Survey and telemetry implementation must explicitly manage lawful basis, transparency, minimization, retention, user rights, and consent quality where consent is used. EDPB consent guidance is the key benchmark in EU contexts, requiring consent to be freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous. For California, users must be informed of rights (including opt-out and correction rights under CPRA updates), and mechanisms such as Global Privacy Control should be respected where applicable.

### Licensing and Certification

There is typically no universal license requirement to operate an AI developer workflow platform in general software markets. However, certification and attestation pathways (for example ISO/IEC 27001 certification and often SOC 2 audits in practice) are strong market credibility and enterprise-procurement enablers. For a master's-thesis study, institutional ethics approval requirements (where applicable at your university) should also be treated as mandatory governance gates before collecting human-subject feedback.

### Implementation Considerations

Implement compliance by design in Chiron's measurement stack: collect only necessary fields, attach purpose metadata to every metric/survey event, maintain retention windows, and provide rights-handling workflows (access/deletion/correction/opt-out). Segment telemetry and survey data by consent/version, and maintain auditable event lineage that can justify claims made in product messaging. Because regulators actively target deceptive AI claims, thesis and marketing statements should be backed by measurable evidence from your mixed-method study.

### Risk Assessment

Primary regulatory risks for Chiron are: (1) over-collection of telemetry without clear lawful basis/purpose mapping, (2) weak consent or notice quality for in-product surveys, (3) inability to fulfill data-subject rights within required timelines, (4) unsupported product claims about productivity/quality/control, and (5) cross-jurisdiction compliance drift as EU and U.S. rules evolve. Mitigation priority should be policy-coded instrumentation, jurisdiction-aware privacy controls, auditable consent/versioning, and evidence-linked claim governance.
